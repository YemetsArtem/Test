Treating erection problems as a medical issue can boost yoursex life and health

More than 50% of all men will experience erectile dysfunction at some point in their lives. There’s a fifty-fifty chance you’re one of them. When weak or short erections turn into a recurring pattern, it becomes a medical issue and calls for treatment.

“OMG! I can’t face the doctor with this!”

The shame of having to admit there’s something wrong is what keeps men from addressing erection problems seriously. “What if the doctor asks questions about my sexual activity?” and: “Will the doctor touch me in my private parts?” Many men cringe at the mere thought of this. But, is there a basis for all these concerns?

What to expect at your doctors' office

A physician is much less interested in a man’s sexual practices than he is in the person’s medical history and overall health. Here are some of the questions the doctor will ask:

  • How long do you experience the problem and did it start suddenly?
  • Are you going through a phase of increased stress, depression or anxiety?
  • Do you get tired quickly when exercising? Suffer palpitations or a stumbling heart?
  • Have you ever had any cardio - vascular problems?
  • Do you feel pain in the urinary tract?
  • Do you smoke or drink alcohol? How much?

After a round of questions, the doctor will usually be able to prescribe a suiting treatment. In a few cases, a specialists advice is required or blood may be taken for some tests:

  • Blood sugar - Diabetes commonly causes erectile dysfunction and both need to be treated independently.
  • Cholesterol - A high level will have a harmful effect on the process that creates an erection.
  • Testosterone - If the hormone testosterone is not sufficiently produced, there’s not enough “fuel” for an erection
